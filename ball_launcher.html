The Challenge

For our third first-year engineering design project, we were tasked with identifying a real-world client with a specific need and designing a solution around their constraints. Our client had mobility restrictions that made it difficult to engage in recreational activities that required reaching, bending, or fine motor control.

We chose to design a mechanical ball launcher — a device that would allow the client to launch a ball for play or exercise without needing to manually throw it. The launcher needed to be operated with minimal physical effort, be safe to use independently, and reliably launch a standard-sized ball a useful distance with adjustable force.

02

Design Approach

Mechanical Concept

The launcher uses a spring-loaded plunger mechanism with a trigger release. Tension is adjustable via a ratcheting system, allowing the user to control launch force without needing to handle the spring directly.

Iterative Prototyping

Three major iterations: initial cardboard mock-up for ergonomics, a 3D-printed proof-of-concept for mechanism validation, and a final refined version with structural reinforcements and tuned spring tension.

User-Centred Design

Regular client consultations shaped every design decision — from handle shape and trigger position to loading mechanism and base stability. The final design accommodates one-handed operation from a seated position.

03

Outcome

The final ball launcher successfully met all client requirements: it can be operated with one hand from a seated position, launches a standard tennis ball between 3 and 10 metres depending on the tension setting, and has a stable base that prevents tipping during use. The client was able to use it independently after a brief demonstration.

The project reinforced the importance of early and frequent client consultation in the design process. The most valuable insights came not from CAD models or simulations, but from watching the client interact with our prototypes and listening to their feedback on what was and wasn't working.

Project Details

This was the third project in the 1P13 Engineering Design sequence at McMaster University. The course emphasizes the full design process: problem identification, client consultation, concept generation, prototyping, testing, and final presentation. Our team received positive feedback from both the client and course instructors on the usability and thoughtful design of the final product.

The experience taught me that the best engineering solutions are not necessarily the most technically complex — they are the ones that best fit the user's actual needs, constraints, and context.
