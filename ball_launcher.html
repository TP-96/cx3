First Year Engineering Design Project 3 · Jan – Apr 2026

Mechanical
Ball Launcher

Designed and built a ball launcher to address a real world issue for a client. Our client had specific mobility restrictions which we based the design around. 1P13 Project 3.

⏱ Jan – Apr 2026 🏫 McMaster University 👥 Team of 4
01

The Challenge

Our client had limited upper body mobility and wanted a simple, mechanical way to play fetch with their dog independently. The device needed to be operated one-handed with minimal force, launch a tennis ball 10–15 metres, and require no electricity or batteries.

The core engineering challenge was achieving consistent ball trajectory and distance using only human mechanical power — no springs under tension, no compressed air, no electronics.

Requirements

Range10–15 metres
OperationOne-handed, ≤5 N force
PowerFully mechanical (no electricity)
Ball SizeStandard tennis ball (6.7 cm)
SafetyNo pinch points, stable base
02

Design Approach

Mechanical Concept

The final design uses a lever-arm mechanism with a torsion spring assist. A pivoting arm stores energy as the user pulls a handle downward; a trigger release converts that stored energy into a smooth throwing motion. The geometry was optimized to deliver consistent launch angle and velocity regardless of how hard the user pulls.

Iterative Prototyping

Three prototypes were built over the 12-week design cycle. Prototype 1 (cardboard + 3D printed joints) proved the lever kinematics. Prototype 2 (plywood frame, metal pivot) tested the spring force curve. Prototype 3 (final materials) delivered the target distance within specification.

User-Centred Design

Our client tested each prototype and provided feedback that directly shaped the design — the handle shape, the trigger location, and the base stability were all adjusted based on their experience. The final device can be operated entirely with one hand while seated.

03

Outcome

The final prototype consistently launched a tennis ball 12 metres with a launch angle of 35°. The trigger pull required approximately 4 N of force — well within the client's capability. The device was delivered to the client with full documentation and maintenance instructions.
