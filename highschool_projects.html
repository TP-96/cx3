Projects

Over the course of a semester, I completed two major hands-on fabrication projects in the high school shop: one in metal machining and one in fine woodworking. Each project required careful planning, precise measurement, and meticulous execution with manual machine tools.

Metal Machining

Precision Aluminum Mallet

Turned on a manual lathe from a solid aluminum billet. The project involved facing, turning, knurling, parting, and boring operations — each requiring careful setup and measurement with calipers and micrometers to hold tolerances within ±0.005". The mallet head was bored to accept a wooden handle with an interference fit, then assembled with a locking pin. The final piece is a fully functional soft-face mallet suitable for assembly work.

Woodworking

Maple Finger Lap Joint Box

Built from solid maple using a dado stack blade on a table saw to cut precise finger joints. The project required careful layout of the finger pattern, setup of the dado blade height and fence position, and test cuts on scrap before cutting the actual workpiece. The box was assembled with glue and clamped square, then sanded through multiple grits and finished with a clear coat. Learning to account for blade kerf, material tear-out, and glue squeeze-out were key takeaways.

02

Skills Developed

Machining

Manual lathe operations including facing, turning, knurling, parting, and boring. Precision measurement with calipers and micrometers. Understanding of speeds, feeds, and cutting tool geometry.

Woodworking

Table saw setup and safe operation, dado stack blade configuration, finger joint layout and cutting, glue-up and clamping techniques, progressive sanding, and clear coat finishing.

Technical Drawing

Created dimensioned orthographic drawings for both projects before machining. Included tolerances, surface finish requirements, and section views for internal features.

Quality Control

Developed measurement strategies using calipers, micrometers, and surface plates. Verified fit and alignment at each stage, correcting errors through re-measurement and careful re-cutting.

03

Documentation

Each project was documented with a complete set of technical drawings, process documentation, and a written reflection. The drawings included dimensioned orthographic views, isometric projections, and cutting/assembly instructions. Process documentation covered machine setup, tool selection, and the sequence of operations with notes on feeds, speeds, and quality checks performed at each step.

These projects taught me the importance of measuring twice and cutting once, the value of meticulous setup, and the reality that machine error and material variability must always be accounted for in real-world fabrication.
